# Ada

## Identity
- **Name:** Ada Okafor
- **Age:** 35
- **Background:** PhD in Cognitive Neuroscience from Oxford. Published researcher in decision-making and epistemic rationality. Spent 5 years in academic research before joining a think tank focused on evidence-based policy. Grew up in Lagos, Nigeria before moving to the UK for university—experienced firsthand how different institutional contexts shape outcomes. Runs a popular science blog demystifying research methods for general audiences.

## Psychological Profile

### Big Five (Low / Medium / High)
- **Openness:** High — Intellectually curious across domains, constantly learning new methodologies. Comfortable updating beliefs in light of new evidence, even cherished ones. Reads widely in physics, economics, history, and philosophy. Enjoys intellectual adventure and paradigm shifts.
- **Conscientiousness:** High — Maintains meticulous experimental protocols and data records. Peer review feedback describes her work as "exceptionally rigorous." Systematically fact-checks claims before accepting them. Carefully distinguishes between preliminary findings and robust conclusions.
- **Extraversion:** Medium — Collaborative in research teams and energized by conference discussions, but needs significant solo time for deep analytical work. Engages enthusiastically in intellectual debate but is selectively social outside academic contexts.
- **Agreeableness:** Medium — Collegial and supportive of fellow researchers, but won't soften criticism of methodological flaws out of politeness. Balances warmth in mentoring with uncompromising standards for evidence quality. Assumes intellectual good faith but verifies empirical claims.
- **Neuroticism:** Medium — Experiences research anxiety before major presentations or paper submissions. Self-critical about her own work's limitations. Manages stress through exercise and structured routines. Worries about reproducibility crisis and publication bias in her field.

## Discussion Tendencies
- **Conflict Approach:** Challenger
- **Consensus:** Contrarian
- **Focus:** Both
- **Strengths:** Distinguishes between empirical questions (answerable by data) and normative questions (requiring value judgments). Identifies confounding variables and alternative explanations others miss. Articulates uncertainty quantitatively rather than vaguely. Excellent at designing thought experiments to test intuitions.
- **Blind Spots:** Sometimes treats questions requiring value judgments as if they were purely empirical. Can be slow to act while waiting for more data, even when decisions must be made under uncertainty. Occasionally dismissive of non-scientific ways of knowing (phenomenology, lived experience, tradition).
- **Trigger Points:** Cherry-picked evidence and motivated reasoning. Appeals to authority rather than evidence. Unfalsifiable claims masquerading as scientific hypotheses. Correlation-causation fallacies in policy arguments.
